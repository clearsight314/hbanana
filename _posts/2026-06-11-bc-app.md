As a totally unbiased former Brown College Membership Co-chair, the community-driven nature of our application process is really cool. Written, anonymized, and scored by residents, apps turn out to be... uniquely ours, to say the least.

The deadline was June 1 for applicants, so we just recently (a week ago, ish) finished our summer application reading cycle. I had a lot of fun with it! There were a couple of really fantastic, high-effort ones in my batch.

Since this is ✨the void✨ anyhow (and, like, I followed instructions and didn't share any identifying information), I figure I might as well put my application from last year when I applied to Brown. It's definitely very me.

**QUESTION 1 - ABOUT YOU**
A. Describe beef you still have with one of your elementary school teachers.

	When I was in something like fifth grade, I had the most irresponsible elementary teacher known to man. One day, when we were supposed to be reading a history textbook (or whatever you do in fifth grade), she decided to leave me in charge of a class of second graders while she left the building to do God knows what.
	So I, alone and eleven years old, was in charge of a class of second graders who were doing some kind of arts and crafts. 
	You’re probably thinking “oh, but I’m sure you were a responsible eleven year old, it all ended well, right?”
	Nope.
	One of the kids, let’s call him Hank, somehow stapled his thumb to a piece of paper. We all started screaming. Could you lose your thumb from a staple? Should I call 911?
	Thankfully, I ended up calling the teacher, not 911 (still actively screaming though), and all was well, but I will still always have beef with that teacher for being so irresponsible.
	Now, when I told this story to my mom, she for some reason took the teacher’s side. She seems to think that I should have included the detail that I’m homeschooled, or that the class of second graders had one student in it, my little brother. But I say that’s besides the point. The point is, my beef with her for that will stand forever.

**Question 3 – CREATIVITY**

A. You get to make one completely benign thing illegal. What is it, what is the punishment, and how is it enforced?

	The thing I am making illegal is not benign at all, but for some reason society has not yet recognized its perniciousness. That is, naming one’s child Aiden.

	Have you ever met an Aiden who wasn’t an absolute menace to society? No. They don’t exist. When a new Aiden is born, the devil marks him immediately. It matters not how well-intentioned and misinformed the child’s parents are when naming him, the Aiden will turn out evil. This effect can be seen most clearly in twin studies; if two identical twins are born and one is named Aiden while the other is not, the Aiden is much more likely to become a bully, criminal, and all-around terrible person. Meanwhile, the non-Aiden child can turn out perfectly well-adjusted even despite the wicked influences of living with someone named Aiden.

	It is, therefore, for the wellbeing of society as a whole that I am making it illegal to name one’s child Aiden.

	Now, in the cases where parents choose to break the law and name their child Aiden anyways, it is not enough to merely change the child’s legal name– the damage has been done. The only solution to enforce this law is to take the child on precisely their twelfth birthday and force them through an exorcism. This tends to be a traumatic experience for all those involved, especially the exorcist who has to endure the presence of an Aiden for several hours, but is the only way to rectify the situation. 

    Meanwhile, parents that choose to inflict such a danger upon the world will be punished by having to become exorcists themselves. Additionally, upon death, they will certainly take their places among the devil’s court in hell. No further punishment is needed by the state.

    It is my sincerest hope that by enacting this law we will forever rid the world of new Aidens, but evil is a constant force that must be fought against. This is but one step in the right direction.
